    Linotype Exhibition

    The linotype, a machine of the 19th century that made the United States the most literate country during the machine's peak, has its origins in Baltimore. Newspapers like The New York Times and The Baltimore Sun were able to drastically lengthen and sensationalize their papers. The Baltimore Museum of Industry (BMI) features this revolutionary machine in demonstrations and as part of one of its permanent exhibits.

    The Original Exhibit

    The original exhibit consists of two adjacent 12 ft high walls and is part of an existing permanent exhibit standing against walls that meet at 45 degree angles. First-time visitors will first observe the right side of the exhibit.

    The Inspiration

    Rather than displaying the linotype in its actual color, I took inspiration from the following image and used the following color palette. The color palette is also reminiscent of the BMI's branding.

    The Design

    Taking inspiration from Pinterest, I wanted to add color to the exhibit. My design is meant to inform and engage users from middle schoolers to adults. Using artifacts and screens, the design teaches visitors about how the machine moved the printing industry forward.

    The Vantage Points

    My design focuses on visitors of all ages (especially middle schoolers) and catches the attention of viewers from the left. The design I proposed utilizes a large TV screen with iPad minis in order to effectively teach users about how innovative this machine was during its time.

    The Interactive Experience

    Multiple middle school students can work together or one adult user can use the iPad minis that together act as the keyboard/interface for the linotype. Users can select a phase of the linotype process: create the lines, create the slugs, and redistribute the molds. The movie screen shows what the linotype does and how it works. If technology cannot work at the time, the left side of the wall displays the artifacts/parts used to make printing happen.
